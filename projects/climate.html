Climate Change Here and Now

ClimateXR is a research project studying the effects of immersive virtual reality on psychological distance towards climate change. As part of Tel Aviv University's Immersive Media and Cognition group at the Sagol Brain Institute at Ichilov Hospital, this project investigates how embodiment in VR influences both our perception of climate change issues and our willingness to take action.

Project Overview

Israel is considered a "Hotspot" in terms of the effects of climate change. Projections show that Israel will experience longer and hotter heat waves. During the winter, compound flooding is expected, a combination of heavy precipitation and strong storm surges in coastal areas. Policies which can reduce the adverse effects of climate change are at hand, but they are currently not fully implemented.

Psychological barriers affect the way in which we tackle climate change. One of these mechanisms is "Psychological distance" in which our brain constructs and reacts differently to events perceived as close or distant to us - either temporally, geographically or socially. Events which are perceived as distant receive less attention and urgency than those perceived as proximal.

The core of the project was built in Unity, creating interactive scenarios that simulated various climate change effects in Israel. Participants experienced these scenarios while wearing VR headsets equipped with neuro-feedback sensors to measure their psychological and physiological responses. This data was then analyzed to understand how the immersive experience influenced their perception of climate change as an immediate versus distant concern.

Research Methodology

Our research utilized a mixed-methods approach combining qualitative and quantitative data collection:

  • Immersive Scenarios: Creating VR experiences that simulated future climate scenarios specific to Israel
  • Physiological Measurements: Collecting real-time neuro-feedback during VR exposure
  • Behavioral Assessment: Measuring willingness to engage in climate action before and after VR exposure
  • Long-term Follow-up: Assessing lasting impacts on psychological distance and behavior change

Participants were randomly assigned to either an immersive VR condition or a control condition using traditional media. By comparing responses between these groups, we could measure the specific impact of embodied VR experiences on climate change perception.

Outcomes & Impact

The ClimateXR project demonstrated several important findings about immersive technology's potential to influence climate perception:

  • Participants who experienced climate scenarios in VR reported a significant reduction in psychological distance to climate change compared to control groups
  • Immersive experiences led to increased reported intentions to engage in pro-environmental behaviors
  • Physiological measurements correlated with self-reported emotional responses, validating the impact of the VR experience
  • The research established a methodology for using XR technologies in environmental psychology research

These findings have implications for climate communication strategies, suggesting that immersive technologies might be valuable tools for engaging the public with climate change issues and potentially motivating action. The research continues to inform ongoing work in environmental psychology and virtual reality applications.
