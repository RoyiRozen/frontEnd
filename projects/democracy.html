Project Overview

When Israel's far-right government announced in January 2023 its intended judicial coup to neutralize the judiciary branch, I gathered a team of 15 developers, designers, and product managers to build a product that got people out into the streets to protest. We launched DemocracyAR, an AR mini-site that made street signs morph into the names of far-right extremists, offering a quick glimpse of what life under a dictatorship might look like.

The effect was strong. People were shocked and amused. One small visualization hack helped turn "dictatorship" into a relatable, and most importantly, actionable concept.

Technology can be a powerful tool for activism when it makes abstract threats tangible. DemocracyAR transformed a complex political situation into a visceral experience that motivated action.

The Challenge

The proposed judicial reforms presented a complex challenge for public engagement:

  • Constitutional changes are often abstract and difficult for the general public to understand
  • The real-world implications of judiciary neutralization were not immediately apparent
  • There was a need to motivate immediate action before changes were implemented
  • Traditional protest methods were facing fatigue and diminishing returns
  • Information overload made it difficult for important messages to break through

We needed a way to cut through the noise, make the abstract concrete, and inspire action. Augmented reality offered a unique opportunity to help people visualize the potential future implications of these legal changes in their everyday environment.

AR Solution

DemocracyAR was designed as a web-based augmented reality experience that required no app download, making it accessible to as many people as possible. The key features included:

Street Sign Transformation
Web-Based AR
Social Sharing
Location Awareness

The core experience worked by:

  1. Using computer vision to identify street signs through the user's camera
  2. Applying real-time 3D mapping to overlay a transformed version of the sign
  3. Replacing street names with the names of far-right political figures
  4. Maintaining the original sign's visual style for a seamless, realistic effect
  5. Offering contextual information about the depicted political figure when tapped

This created a powerful "what if" scenario that helped people visualize their neighborhoods under a different political reality, making the abstract threat immediate and personal.

Hackathon Development

The project was developed during an intensive hackathon focused on tech for democracy. As team leader, I:

  • Assembled a multidisciplinary team of 15 volunteers within 48 hours of the judicial announcement
  • Coordinated remote and in-person collaboration across multiple time zones
  • Established clear roles and responsibilities to ensure efficient progress
  • Facilitated rapid prototyping and testing cycles
  • Managed the transition from prototype to production-ready application

Team Composition

Development Team

UX/UI designers, visual designers, and 3D modelers

Social media strategists, content creators, and community organizers

The hackathon format created a sense of urgency and purpose, allowing us to move from concept to working prototype in just 72 hours. The winning project was then refined over the next week for public release.

Technical Implementation

Creating a seamless AR experience that worked reliably on standard smartphones required several technical innovations:

WebXR
Computer Vision
Three.js
Node.js Backend

Key technical challenges we overcame included:

  • Developing image recognition algorithms specific to street sign detection
  • Creating a lightweight AR experience that worked on older mobile devices
  • Ensuring text replacement maintained linguistic context and readability
  • Building a responsive interface that worked across various screen sizes
  • Implementing analytics to measure user engagement and protest conversions

The application was built using web technologies to avoid app store approval processes and ensure immediate availability, crucial for time-sensitive activism.

User Experience Design

For DemocracyAR to be effective as an activist tool, the user experience needed to be:

  • Immediately Accessible: No download required, working directly from web browsers
  • Intuitively Obvious: The purpose and function clear within seconds of use
  • Emotionally Impactful: Creating a visceral response that motivated action
  • Socially Shareable: Easy to capture and share the experience with others
  • Call-to-Action Oriented: Clear pathways to participate in protests

The interface was deliberately minimalist, putting focus entirely on the transformed environment rather than on complex UI elements. Educational content was layered, allowing both quick scanning and deeper engagement for those seeking more information.

Leadership Lessons

Leading this project during a politically charged atmosphere taught valuable lessons about using technology for social impact:

  • Speed vs. Perfection: Recognizing when "good enough now" beats "perfect later" in time-sensitive activism
  • Cross-Disciplinary Collaboration: Creating effective frameworks for technologists, designers, and activists to work together
  • Ethical Considerations: Balancing impactful messaging against potential polarization
  • Technical Accessibility: Ensuring solutions work for the broadest possible audience
  • Measuring Impact: Developing appropriate metrics for activism technology beyond traditional engagement measurements

The experience reinforced the power of technology to make abstract concepts tangible, and the responsibility that comes with creating tools that shape public understanding and action.

In times of political crisis, the most important technology isn't necessarily the most advanced—it's the technology that makes complex realities understandable and actionable for ordinary citizens.
